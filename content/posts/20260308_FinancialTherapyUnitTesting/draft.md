Avoidance, not fear
Problem with the system
"monitoring" leads to better results

I don't trust [Simplifi](link), or any other of the financial management software.  So, for a few years, I tried just maintaining a vast spreadsheet where I recorded every dollar spent, and used it to forecast where we'd be in a few months.  You know, future planning.

That spreadsheet was a lot of work though.  I found myself spending all my time doing just the accounting part, and very little on the future planning.  So, I stopped trusting that system as well.  Plus the work was too much friction - it was too easy to just stop maintaining it.






I've tried various systems to monitor and control my finances.  The primary goal was to keep my wife happy and secure, and the secondary goal was to get filthy rich of course.  Let's just say that, hitherto, the latter goal has not yet been met, and the former is doing only a little better.

Since all the systems I've tried take a lot of elbow grease and other fiddling to get clear answers from, they typically evolve into systems of avoidance instead of clarity

Recently I tried something new, built off all the lessons I've learned over the past few years of trying to thwart various hinderances built up over a lifetime.

I resolved to text my wife every morning, tell her our current liquid cash balance, and our largest expense of the past 24 hours.  To keep myself on track, I installed a nice little app on my phone, [Habit Loop](link).  Now, whenever I send her the text, I just click the icon on my phone, and get a little hit of good by seeing the icon turn from black to green.

I found that resolving to be transparent with my wife on a daily basis really helped get on track with a better system.  Most importantly, it felt like I was building trust within my family.

Then, I missed a big bill.  My initial response was to panic - we had already allocated that money to something else.  My next response was to drop my morning habit.  When my wife and I next met to talk finances, the tension was pretty high.  We had to do some scrambling to move things around, which we did.  And then it became clear that . what.  it's too fucking late to keep writing this thing?

yes, it is.





The reason I'm such a flake on planning my family's financial future is because I'm just scared.  Fear of the future is virtually preventing me from planning more than a few days ahead.  Any further, and "I don't know if we have the money for that...  Close your eyes, hope for the best.  Sound Confident."

But that not only leads to a breakdown every few weeks, it also leaves my wife wondering if she hitched her wagon to the wrong horse.

A few weeks ago, I came up with an idea (during my [Morning Pages](LINK) of course).  The idea was that, since the blockage comes from a FEAR, I should be able to deal with that in the same way as any other fear - exposure therapy!

Using all the tricks I've learned up to now (atomic habits, minimal friction, adding links to the chain, etc.), I came up with a plan.  Every morning, I'll text my wife our current bank balance, and the largest expense of the past 24 hours.  The effect should be that I'm lowering my resistance to knowing our financial situation, and talking to my wife about it, day by day.

To help keep on track, I found a cool app called "Habit Loop".  You basically create a "habit" you want to start (e.g. morning balance), and then click it every day when you do it.  You can put a little one-icon widget on your phone, and just hit it when you do your thing.

It worked great!  Once, when I was in the dog house, my wife said something like "I don't know why you keep sending me our balance, it doesn't help."  But I kept with it, day after day, *trusting the process*.  And I started to get more on top of the money, and better able to plan for the future.

Then for some reason I stopped doing it.  I guess I just got over confident or something.  Oh, I remember - I missed a massive bill that was coming up this month, and actually *lost* confidence.  Whatever.  The point is, I stopped, and the trust bridge I was building between my finances and my wife started to break down.
